Peer Support Among Youth on Social Media

Human-Computer Interaction | UX Research

June - August 2022

The STAR (Students Tackling Advanced Research) Program marks some of my memorable first-times: my first job and my first academic research. This program of Pennoni Honors College, Drexel University, is an opportunity for first-year students to engage in an early faculty-mentored undergraduate research over the summer. I collaborated with two faculty professors to study how teenagers support each other on Instagram Direct Messaging with the main role in directly coding and analyzing the provided data. Our research paper got accepted to be published at the ACM CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems in April, 2023. This research was also the groundwork for our current study on youth’s norms and perspectives on effective peer mentoring (from September 2022 to the present).

1. Literature Review: Other researchers' footprints

Despite enjoying working with real data and information more than reading research papers, I still found myself gaining a lot of insights into the problem we were studying through literature review. To my surprise, most of the mentoring programs for teens were in-person and provided by adults and professionals. Meanwhile, social media and private messaging platforms, which are potentially an accessible source of support for youth, hadn’t been well studied.

Identifying the knowledge gap, we decided on 2 research questions we wanted to address:

  1. What are the topics/problems for which youth sought support?
  2. What are the types of support they received via private messaging platforms?

2. Data Coding: Understanding the Teenagers

In this project, we were authorized to utilize some resources from one of my mentors’ previous study: a codebook of support types and a dataset of over 500 Instagram conversations among teenagers (their identity and personal information was confidential). A secured web platform was set up for the access of the dataset. I went through the dataset and filtered out 82 conversations that were relevant to our scope of study. The data coding process was executed on Microsoft Excel: I read every single conversation and coded them based on the support type codebook (what kind of support the participants used, how they comforted each other specifically with words and actions). Working on the data, I developed a new codebook for the conversations’ topics, or the problems participants were dealing with.

Throughout the coding process, I noted down some interesting pieces of information outside our scope of study that can give us more insights into the research results later on:

  1. How teens initiated peer support conversations (checking in, starting with casual conversations, etc)
  2. The relationships between teens (friends, romantic partners, strangers, etc)
  3. Teens' negative reaction to help seekers (refusing to help, blaming, etc)
  4. Teens' negative reaction to help providers (rejecting help, disrespecting, etc)
  5. Mutual support (roles of help seekers and help providers are interchangeable)

3. Data Analysis: Numbers in Qualitative Research

We used tables and charts to visualize our coded data, according to support types and subtypes, topics and subtopics. Besides, we analyzed the conversations based on categories such as: relevancy to the study, mutual support, whether mentees were happy and satisfied with the support, and so on. Having a mix of unarranged different charts and tables in hands, we faced a challenge as to how we could combine them into research results. This process was frustrating at first as we were overwhelmed with information, but once we eliminated the insignificant numbers and focused on the main categories, we were amazed at the findings we got. As the main research assistant to work with the data throughout the project, I found it interesting to step into the participants’ world, get to know their problems and sympathize with them. This is the first time I can understand the users’ stories from their perspective, instead of a researcher or designer’s point of view. I believe this is also an important part of UX research, in which researchers learn about their users with sympathy and compassion, and connect with them on a personal level.

4. Not just a Summer Research

At the end of the summer, there was a summer showcase in which every STAR scholar had their poster hung up and presented about their research to the audience. I prepared a research poster for the on-campus fair and a video presentation for the virtual showcase. This is the time when I got a chance to reflect on what I had been doing over the summer, what I did well and what not. My mentors and I also wrote a paper based on our research findings and got it published at the ACM CHI conference - the premier international conference of Human-Computer Interaction. Although I wasn’t in charge of the writing process, it was a great opportunity for me to get involved in building an academic paper and understand how the process was going.

This summer research's results have inspired us to work on a new project, studying young adults' needs and design requirements for effective online social support and support exchange systems. We are preparing to recruit research participants to conduct user surveys and interviews. Our end goal is to create a design solution that improves teens' experience with peer support.

As a newbie researcher, I’m happy with what I have done throughout the program. I got to learn more about research, an important branch in my major of study, and become interested in it - which I have never considered the case as I’m more into the design track. Moreover, I’m excited to continue with this project after the STAR program as I’m designing a prototype for a digital platform that can improve teenagers’ experience with peer support.

Kaylie is a UI/UX designer and researcher based in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania.
