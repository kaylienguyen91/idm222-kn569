Sharing Excess Wholesale App

Mobile App Design | UX Design | UX Research

July 2023 - September 2023

I redesigned the Wholesale mobile app of Sharing Excess - a nonprofit addressing the pressing issue of food insecurity in the United States - to streamline their process of working with input and output data in the warehouse, which significantly shortened the process, minimized human errors, and increased workers' productivity. This experience also marked as my first time leading UX of a business with a fellow UX designer.

Founded in Philadelphia in 2018, Sharing Excess has rescued and delivered more than 25 million pounds of food to communities and organizations, expanding its impact across 20 states in America.

Collecting food then Delivering it - What is complex about it?

To examine the real process behind, I conducted a Field Study and shadowed 3 Food Sourcing Associates of Sharing Excess through a typical workday in the warehouse.

Around 30k pounds of food is received and distributed from 9AM to 11AM everyday - a fast and chaotic process. And the real frustrations lied in working with the data.

The existing Tool Didn't Do What It was Supposed to Do

Sharing Excess (SE) has a mobile app for the company-wide internal workflow, including the Wholesale section taking care of the Food Sourcing process. However, it didn't allow workers to record data in real time right in the warehouse, due to numerous shortcomings:

1. It lacks most of the important information

The Entry form had no fills to enter important numbers such as case count and case weight.

⇒ They put all these numbers into "Notes" fill

⇒ Disorganized and confusing

2. It can't do the math for users

For example, to transfer the total weight to case count (i.e. the number of cases), workers have to manually calculate it then enter into the app.

⇒ Time-consuming while the process has to be quick

⇒ Stress, human error, data inaccuracy

3. It doesn't have reminders or alert messages

There are situations in which food is not distributed to organizations on the same day it arrives SE warehouse. On the next day, workers may forget about it, and the app doesn't show the entries of previous days or remind them about the food stuck in warehouse.

⇒ The food loses quality and isn't qualified to be distributed ⇒ Wasteful

Seeing tons of other technical issues with the app, I learned that how the app worked technically didn’t align with users’ work process in real time at all.
It wasn't designed to serve its users.

Workers had a Temporary Solution, but It Wasn't Optimal

Getting frustrated with the app, workers in the warehouse created a Google Sheets as their main working platform in real time: add and organize every information they find important, use formulas for calculation, sort/filter data, etc.

However, they also understood that they couldn't use this platform forever:

  • The SE app is the main "workplace" of the whole company, as well as its donors and organizations. It can't be replaced.
  • They still had to transfer data from the sheet to the app at the end of the day, which didn't shorten the process.

In an ideal world, Tate - one of the main Food Sourcing Associates - hoped to get rid of 1.5 mind-numbing hours of moving data between 2 platforms, and use the SE app right in the warehouse.

Problem Statement

The SE app is not a well-functioning and optimal all-in platform, therefore users have to utilize an external platform to do their work. This prolongs and overcomplicates the process, thus negatively affecting employees' work productivity and outcomes.

Listen to the Users' honest Thoughts

After the Field Study with 3 Food Sourcing associates in warehouse, I still felt the need to have a more in-depth conversation with them to identify problems in detail and explore potential solutions. Thus, I set up a Focus Group and UX Workshop with all 3 workers:

  • Asked users to list every problem with the work process and the app, then order them in terms of priority and urgency.
  • Asked them to compare the pros and cons of SE app and Google Sheets
  • Held a group discussion to elaborate on their answers and brainstorm their ideal solutions.

There were frustrations that I didn't see until I talked to the users: e.g. no standard naming convention for the process; so many unexpected scenarios that the app couldn't cover.

Working in an environment requiring speed and concentration, they don’t want to be perplexed by how the app works ⇒ something intuitive and self-explanatory even for beginners.

Their insights and suggestions gave me a direction to move forward in the design process: the key is to make the flow of the app replicate the real-time process and implement advantageous features of Google Sheets into the app.

"How Might We..."

How might we integrate every function users need into SE App to streamline the process of working with Food Sourcing input and output data?

Overview of Design Iterations

The workers' Food Sourcing process was complicated, and our design process wasn't linear either. Sometimes one problem was solved but then another one arose.

The biggest challenge lied in communicating with users to thoroughly understand their process and frustrations, and communicating with developers to ensure our intentions behind every design decision would be conveyed in the app.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes

I separated Entry and Allocation processes into 2 views that can be accessed individually, instead of having Allocation nested inside Entry as in the original app.

⇒ Workers can easily navigate the task they want to do

Final Design: High-Fidelity Screens

1. Enter data in an organized and logical way

  • Add fills for every important number to store data individually
  • ⇒ No longer write every numbers in Notes fill
  • Create "Before Sorting" and "After Sorting" tabs in Entry form; tailor the design of each tab to replicate the real-life process
  • ⇒ Separate different types of data
  • ⇒ Avoid confusion and mistakes

2. Distribute multiple products to an organization effectively

  • Original app: create one form for each type of product that an organization orders
  • New app: add multiple types of products into a form for an organization
  • ⇒ Group related data together ⇒ Easy to keep track
  • ⇒ Shorten the process and save time

3. Be reminded to distribute all donated food

  • Have a separate page for Remaining Produce to list out foods donated days ago but haven’t been distributed
  • Display older foods in red
  • ⇒ Ensure they are still in good condition and distributed ASAP
  • ⇒ Minimize food waste and increase the company's impacts

4. Stop doing math manually

  • Activate auto-calculation for related data
  • Example: Case Count * Case Weight = Total Weight
  • ⇒ Entering any 2 of these 3 numbers will have the other one calculated
  • ⇒ Avoid human error and increase data accuracy

Workers can now do their work at ease

1.5 hours ⇒ 0 hour

of transfering data between 2 platforms. Workers don't need Google Sheets anymore.

1-2 minutes ⇒ 30 seconds

of entering data for one Entry/Allocation form. Workers can confidently use the SE app in real time.

75%

reduction of human error and data inaccuracy. In-app calculation and Alert messages help workers avoid mistakes.

60%

reduction of food waste. Workers are reminded to distribute food in a timely manner.

What I learned over the project

1. Put myself into users’ shoes

To solve complex problems of the app, I had to work closely with the users, and even build a relationship with them. User research is not only setting up an interview with people, but also “visiting” them and observing their frustrations in real life.

2. Evaluate my design decisions from multiple perspectives

Every time testing my designs with the workers, I also handed them off to stakeholders and developers. They would let me know if the design was codable or if it might cause any problem.

3. Take the initiative and Learn along the way

I was nervous to know that another intern and I would be the only designers of the company. I read more books, consulted other UX professionals, and self-studied to gain confidence. Thus I soon worked my way out and took control of the project.
