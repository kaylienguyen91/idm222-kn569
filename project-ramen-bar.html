Ramen Bar Website Redesign

Responsive Web | UI Design

September 2021 - March 2022

Being a loyal customer of Ramen Bar - a local Japanese restaurant in University City, Philadelphia - I identified some of their website’s design problems that negatively affects mine (and other users') experience with the restaurant, no matter how amazing their foods are. As a designer who desires to create solutions for people’s problems, I decided to redesign Ramen Bar’s website with the aim to improve their customers’ experience.

1. Project Definition: Users’ unmet demands

“Why does the restaurant make it so hard for me to give them money?”

This is the question I heard the most while interviewing other Ramen Bar’s customers. Navigating a restaurant’s website, they just want to view the menu and make an online order in the easiest and fastest way possible. However, based on the users’ comments and my own research on the original website, Ramen Bar was doing the opposite:

  • Disengaging layout and no visual aids on Menu page (the most fundamental page) to draw users in
  • Lack of necessary information such as nutrition, ingredients on the Menu
  • No direct way to make an online order on the website

Understanding the gap between the users’ needs and the website’s performance, I laid out the main user tasks I needed to focus on and the goals I wanted to achieve at the end of the project.

2. Information Architecture and Research: Listen to the Users

I asked six target users to complete a card sorting exercise by categorizing different types of content. Being curious about their perspectives, I had a short interview with each of them after the exercise to investigate what kind of content they expect to see on the site, and the rationale behind how they categorize information. Those insightful answers challenged my assumptions about the ideal hierarchy for the site - we are all the website’s target audience but our opinions could still be different.

3. Wireframing: Preliminary but Thoughtful Ideas

This is my favorite part of the project, in which I transformed intangible research insights into tangible design potentials. With pencil and paper, I sketched out the web pages with a question in mind: how to streamline users’ process of completing their tasks on the site? I invited the same users from the card sorting exercise to test the paper prototype. Their constructive feedback on the user flow showed me how a simple, straightforward design can be more impactful than a fancy one.

Moving from desktop to tablet and mobile devices, I deliberated how the content and design elements should be shifted between screens to maintain consistency and responsiveness. Small design details emphasizing accessibility, such as how big the buttons should be in order to be clickable on mobile screens, were also paid close attention to.

4. UI Design: Accessibility and Consistency

Having got a solid structure for the web pages, I started building the design system. Sticking to the restaurant’s simple but elegant branding colors, I decided to add some new colors to the palette - yellow, orange, green - inspired by the image of sushi. These colors helped highlight key features and bring a more vibrant atmosphere to the design.

The addition of visual aids was the game changer of the website. Since “a picture is worth a thousand words” and the customers eat with their eyes first, I incorporated real images into every dish on the menu with the aim to draw the users in. My testers commented on how engaging the new website with visual aids is, compared to the original site with mostly text.

5. That’s not the End of the Project (yet)

UX design is an iterative and never-ending process. I believe there’s no perfect end result of a project - there’ll be problems arising over the time. That’s why recently (April 2023) I’ve decided to continue refining the design even though the project was supposed to be finished in March 2022. I’ve been going back and forth between improving my design, asking for feedback and having the prototype tested. It's still a work in progress, but I have learned be resilient and patient until I reach my end goal.

The project's result is the site’s most important pages on three devices, showing considerable improvement in the design and flexibility in transition between distinct screen sizes. The whole process also helped enhance my design skills and confidence in UI design. Getting multiple positive and constructive opinions from people, the project laid the groundwork for my future projects.
