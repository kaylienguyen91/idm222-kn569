Ramen Bar Website Redesign

Responsive Web | UI/UX Design

September 2021 - March 2022

Being a loyal customer of Ramen Bar - a local Japanese restaurant in University City, Philadelphia - I identified some of their website’s design problems that negatively affects my (and other users') experience with the restaurant, no matter how amazing their foods are. As a designer who desires to create solutions for people’s problems, I decided to redesign Ramen Bar’s website with the aim to improve their customers’ experience.

 1.  “Why does the restaurant make it so hard for me to give them money?”

This is the question I heard the most while interviewing other Ramen Bar’s customers. Navigating a restaurant’s website, they just want to view the menu and make an online order in the easiest and fastest way possible. However, based on the users’ comments and my own research on the original website, Ramen Bar was doing the opposite:

Understanding the gap between the users’ needs and the website’s performance, I laid out the main user tasks I needed to focus on and the goals I wanted to achieve at the end of the project.

 2.  Users think Differently than Restaurant Owners

I asked six target users to complete a card sorting exercise by categorizing different types of content. Being curious about their perspectives, I had a short interview with each of them to dig deeper into the rationale behind how they categorize information.

Surprisingly, their desired hierarchy turned out to be considerably different from the original website's structure. Those insightful answers also challenged my assumptions about the users - we are all the website’s target audience but our opinions could still be different.

 3.  Simplicity Over Complexity

This is my favorite part of the project, in which I transformed intangible research insights into tangible design potentials. With pencil and paper, I sketched out the web pages with a question in mind:

How to streamline users’ process of completing main tasks on the site?

I invited the same users from the card sorting exercise to test the paper prototype. Their constructive feedback on the user flow showed me how a simple, straightforward design can be more impactful than a fancy one.

Moving from desktop to tablet and mobile devices, I treated them as a completely new design: playing around with creativity to create some eye-catching visual elements. However, not until my testers commented on the discrepancy between screen sizes did I realize my huge design mistake.

In the second round, I deliberated how the content and design elements should be shifted between screens to maintain consistency and responsiveness. Moreover, I started paying attention to accessibility, such as how big the buttons should be in order to be clickable on mobile screens.

 4.  Colors and Images - Visual Elements that trigger the Sense of Taste

Having got a solid structure for the web pages, I started building the design system. Sticking to the restaurant’s simple but elegant branding colors, I decided to add some new colors to the palette - yellow, orange, green - inspired by the image of sushi. These colors helped highlight key features and bring a more vibrant atmosphere to the design.

The addition of visual aids was the game changer of the website.
Since the customers eat with their eyes first, I incorporated real images into every dish on the menu with the aim to draw the users in. My testers commented on how engaging the new website with visual aids is, compared to the original site with mostly text.

 5.  Users have now enjoyed using the website

I asked for final feedback from users whom I was working with throughout the project to document the change in their opinions on the website.

All 6 users recognized a huge improvement in visual design and content layout of the site. Another major feature of the redesigned site is the function of making online orders, which got positive comments from the users.

 6.  That’s not the End of the Story yet

UX design is an iterative and never-ending process. I believe there’s no perfect end result of a project - there’ll be problems arising over the time. That’s why recently (April 2023) I’ve decided to continue refining the design even though the project was supposed to be finished in March 2022. I’ve been going back and forth between improving my design, asking for feedback and having the prototype tested. It's still a work in progress, but I have learned to be resilient and patient until I reach my end goal.

Through my first hands-on experience, I have learned the basics of UX design process: (conduct user research, design wireframes and prototypes). And my biggest lesson: Design for people, and with people. I have practiced not putting my assumptions or personal preference into the design process. I want to apply these lessons to the continuing project of improving this design and future projects in class and internships.
